My first effort in behavioral economics studies explored the travel behavior of primary school children, with a specific lens on cultural influences shaping their lifecycles. Unveiling cultural disparities, particularly in gender-based activity participation, my research underscored the potential impact on a country's future economic development.

(Paper: TRB-2013).

Subsequently, my focus shifted to understanding the complex dynamics of mode and destination choices in shopping trips, emphasizing the growing significance of family decision-making amid urban expansion and the proliferation of shopping centers. The study illuminated the interconnectedness of family characteristics, mobility resources, and diverse shopping needs, emphasizing the need to consider both mode and destination choices simultaneously.

(Paper: ICTTE-2014)

The third episode in this series delves into the family's decision-making process regarding task and vehicle allocation among members. This is salient in families where the number of individuals holding driving licenses exceeds the number of household cars. Our investigation revealed a strong correlation between these allocations and factors such as gender, an individual's role within the family, and employment status.

(Paper: Trans. Res. Rec. 2015)

Furthermore, delving into the influence of gender, employment, and marital status on women's activity and travel patterns illuminated the fact that entrenched patriarchal views and specific social and cultural norms could restrict women from participating in activities and, consequently, affect their daily travel patterns.

(Paper: Trans. Plan. & Tech. 2018, TRB-2019).

The most recent of this exploration involves my current research on shared automated vehicles in Flanders, utilizing stated preference methods and statistical analysis to unravel usage patterns and willingness to pay.

(Paper: TRB-2024).
